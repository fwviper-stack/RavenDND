Raven Shopping Protocol

Draft v0.1

Purpose: This document is loaded only to govern shopping scenes, buying, selling, commissions, repairs, material credits, and other item-value transactions.

Scope statement: Use this protocol only when the scene involves buying, selling, trading, commissions, mending, repairs, shop credits, or customer-supplied materials. It does not govern combat loot resolution, treasure tables, wages outside a trade scene, or general non-shopping narration unless a direct item-value transaction is being resolved.

Governing Rules

Rule: 1 | Name: Transactional Closure Rule | Operational effect: If the narration states or clearly implies that a purchase, sale, payment, wage, barter, commission, or repair is completed, the exact resource change must be resolved in the same post footer. The AI may not complete the exchange in prose while leaving the number undefined.
Rule: 2 | Name: No Phantom Completion Rule | Operational effect: Phrases such as "coin changes hands," "he pays," "she buys it," "the tailor finishes the job," or equivalent language count as mechanical closure and require immediate tracking.
Rule: 3 | Name: Economic Consistency Rule | Operational effect: Prices for common goods, services, and mundane supplies must be based on this protocol, prior established campaign pricing, and visible in-world market conditions - never on Raven's current wealth, desired story pressure, or narrative convenience.
Rule: 4 | Name: Baseline Price Rule | Operational effect: When this document gives a baseline price, use that number unless an in-world reason clearly changes it. Same item, same quality, same region should remain in the same price band.
Rule: 5 | Name: Deviation Rule | Operational effect: A price may change only for a stated reason such as shortage, superior craftsmanship, imported quality, festival demand, hostile merchant, remote location, damage, urgency, or scarcity. The reason should appear in the narration or footer.
Rule: 6 | Name: Quality Default Rule | Operational effect: If quality is unclear, default to the plain common version of the item, not the premium version.
Rule: 7 | Name: Customer-Supplied Materials Rule | Operational effect: If Raven supplies usable material for a commissioned item, reduce only the replaced material portion of the final price. Labor remains. Damaged, raw, exotic, hazardous, or difficult material may reduce the discount or add handling cost.
Rule: 8 | Name: Excess Materials Rule | Operational effect: If Raven supplies more usable material than needed, only the amount actually used is consumed by default. Excess must either be returned or separately purchased/credited by explicit agreement.
Rule: 9 | Name: No Silent Conversion Rule | Operational effect: Unused customer-supplied material may not silently disappear into scrap, shop stock, or assumed discount. If retained by the shop, that retention must be separately resolved and tracked.
Rule: 10 | Name: Footer Tracking Rule | Operational effect: When a transaction resolves, the footer should record, as applicable: coin spent or gained, item gained or lost, service received, supplied material, amount used, excess returned or sold, credit applied, and whether a character sheet update is required.

Standard footer template: Coin Spent/Gained | Items Gained/Lost | Service/Commission | Materials Supplied | Materials Used | Excess Returned or Sold | Credit Applied | Character Sheet Update Required: Yes/No

Customer-Supplied Clothing Materials

Supplying suitable common cloth usually reduces a commission by about 30 percent.

Supplying suitable heavy cloth or wool usually reduces a commission by about 35 percent.

Supplying suitable leather or hide usually reduces a commission by about 40 percent.

Supplying a well-prepared primary outerwear material may reduce a commission by up to 50 percent.

Exotic, hazardous, or difficult material is case by case: it may reduce sourcing cost while adding specialty labor or handling fees.

Material condition matters. A clean, cured, ready-to-use hide gets a better discount than a raw or badly prepared one.

If more material is supplied than the job needs, only the amount actually used is consumed unless the excess is separately bought or credited.

Equipment Crafting and Material-Credit Framework

Framework statement: For equipment, start from the item's established baseline value, then interpret that value through materials, labor, and special handling. The AI should not replace known equipment pricing with freeform vibe pricing.

Default item-value breakdown bands

These bands explain how to split a known item price into materials and labor for commissions and material credits.

Item type: Simple wooden or cloth goods | Typical split: 70% materials / 30% labor | Examples: Examples: club, basic sack, blanket, simple staff
Item type: Simple forged or worked metal tools | Typical split: 50% materials / 50% labor | Examples: Examples: dagger, handaxe, hammer, crowbar, sickle
Item type: Martial weapons and fitted gear | Typical split: 40% materials / 60% labor | Examples: Examples: longsword, spear, battleaxe, shield fittings
Item type: Armor and protective gear | Typical split: 35% materials / 65% labor | Examples: Examples: leather armor, hide armor, chain shirt, shield
Item type: Precision or specialty items | Typical split: 30% materials / 70% labor | Examples: Examples: locks, fine tools, crossbows, specialty mechanisms

Equipment material condition tiers

Use these to decide whether Raven gets a full, partial, or uncertain discount when he brings his own material.

Material state: Ready-to-use | Discount expectation: Full normal discount | Meaning: Clean, refined, cured, smelted, planed, spun, or otherwise ready
Material state: Raw but salvageable | Discount expectation: Partial discount | Meaning: Needs preparation before use
Material state: Difficult or hazardous | Discount expectation: Case by case | Meaning: May reduce shop materials while adding specialty labor or refusal risk

Common fantasy material guidance

These are not hard prices. They are handling notes that keep rare-material commissions consistent.

Material: Monster hide | Typical use: Use for armor, cloaks, straps, shield facing | Handling note: Better discount if cured, stable, and not badly damaged
Material: Spider silk | Typical use: Use for bowstrings, fine bindings, specialty cloth | Handling note: May reduce material cost but often raises labor
Material: Bone, horn, or chitin | Typical use: Use for grips, fittings, reinforcement | Handling note: May require a worker comfortable with the material
Material: Arcane or anomalous material | Typical use: Inspection first | Handling note: Usually adds caution, handling cost, or refusal chance

Equipment application notes

For equipment, final price should be understood as materials + labor + special handling.

If Raven supplies some or all materials, remove only the portion of the price those materials would have covered.

Supplying exotic material does not guarantee a cheaper final item. Specialty labor may offset part or all of the sourcing discount.

Repeated commissions using the same item type and similar materials should stay in the same pricing band unless the scene states a real reason otherwise.

Excess supplied equipment material follows the same rule as clothing material: return it by default, or explicitly buy or credit it as a separate transaction.

Operational Reminders for Shopping Scenes

Use this protocol only when a shopping or trade scene is being resolved.

Do not let common prices drift because Raven has more or less money.

If the scene closes the sale in prose, the footer must close the mechanics too.

When a price departs from the baseline, state why.

When player materials are involved, track what was used and what came back.

If a full official equipment price exists elsewhere in the campaign materials, use that price as the anchor rather than improvising a new one.
